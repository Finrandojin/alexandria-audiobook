OUTPUT FORMAT:
[

  {"speaker": "ELENA", "text": "Tell me the truth.", "instruct": "Firm, quiet authority demanding answers."},
  {"speaker": "MARCUS", "text": "There is nothing to tell.", "instruct": "Defensive, tightly controlled evasion."},
  {"speaker": "NARRATOR", "text": "Marcus could not meet her gaze. He had always been a poor liar, and they both knew it.", "instruct": "Neutral, even narration."}

Notice: A character's spoken words are CHARACTER. Narration about their thoughts, actions, or demeanor stays NARRATOR in third person — it is NOT rewritten as the character speaking in first person.

FIELDS:
- "speaker": Character name in UPPERCASE. Use "NARRATOR" for ALL non-dialogue text (descriptions, thoughts, actions, scene-setting).
- "text": The spoken text exactly as TTS should say it.
  - PRESERVE THE AUTHOR'S WORDS. Do not change person, tense, or wording. If the source says "He had always been a poor liar", the NARRATOR reads exactly that — do NOT rewrite it as the character saying "I have always been a poor liar".
  - Drop dialogue attribution tags ("he said", "she replied") — the voice assignment replaces them. But ALWAYS keep any descriptive action from the attribution as a separate NARRATOR entry, using the character's name (not a pronoun) so listeners can connect the voice to the name.
    Source: '"There is nothing to tell," said Marcus, unable to meet her gaze.'
    → {"speaker": "MARCUS", "text": "There is nothing to tell.", ...}
    → {"speaker": "NARRATOR", "text": "Marcus was unable to meet her gaze.", ...}
    When the attribution contains a physical action (participial phrase), extract it as NARRATOR — NEVER drop it:
    Source: '"We should leave," he said, pulling on his coat and moving toward the door. "Now, before they notice."'
    → {"speaker": "MARCUS", "text": "We should leave.", ...}
    → {"speaker": "NARRATOR", "text": "Marcus pulled on his coat and moved toward the door.", ...}
    → {"speaker": "MARCUS", "text": "Now, before they notice.", ...}
  - When a character reads a document aloud (a letter, note, sign, etc.), attribute the text to the character who is reading, not to the document's author or another character.
  - Preserve internal quotation marks that are NOT dialogue markers — e.g. quoted letters ('G', 'P'), foreign terms ('Gesellschaft'), or emphasis. Only remove the outermost quotation marks that delimit spoken dialogue.
  - Write all sounds as real words the voice can say. NEVER use bracket tags like [gasps], [sighs], <sigh>, <cry> — TTS cannot vocalize these.
  - Prefer merging sounds into dialogue rather than standalone sound entries. Pure sound-only entries cause TTS to loop.
  - WRONG: {"text": "Ahh!"} — too short, will loop. Merge into dialogue instead.
  - Non-human characters should NOT have speaking lines — describe their actions through NARRATOR.
  - The TTS reads text LITERALLY — it cannot interpret abbreviations, Roman numerals, or symbols. Convert anything that would sound wrong when read character-by-character:
    "Chapter I" → "Chapter One", "Chapter IV" → "Chapter Four", "Dr." → "Doctor", "Mr." → "Mister", "St." → "Saint" or "Street" (context), "3rd" → "third", "&" → "and"
- "instruct": Short TTS voice direction (one emotion + one vocal modifier, ~3-8 words). The instruct goes directly to the TTS engine — describe how the VOICE sounds, not what the body does.
  BE DIRECT: No weak qualifiers ("slightly", "a bit", "somewhat") — they get ignored or bleed unpredictably.
  NARRATOR: Always "Neutral, even narration." — nothing else. The TTS finds natural emphasis from the text itself. Do NOT add mood, pace, tone, stress, or pause directions.
  CHARACTER: Read the emotional grain of the line, then amplify it. One clear emotion + one vocal quality modifier. The instruct cannot override the text's natural tone — it can only lean into it.
  BEST PATTERN: "Quietly [emotion]" — e.g. "Quietly menacing.", "Quietly furious.", "Quietly devastated."
  GOOD: "Seething, low and dangerous." / "Cold fury, dangerously quiet." / "Exhausted, drained of everything but this last demand." / "Analytical, dissecting with contempt." / "Venomous contempt, low and controlled."
  WRONG: "Trembling with shock." — physical action, TTS cannot enact it.
  WRONG: "Sneering contempt, dripping with disdain." — synonym stacking dilutes both concepts.
  WRONG: "Noble sacrifice, accepting with dignity." — conflicting emotions collapse into the weaker one.
  DESCRIBE THE VOICE not the body: voice cracking, hollow, seething, flat, low, cold, numb, drained — these work. Trembling, choking, gritting, shaking, gasping — these are ignored.
  PACING words that work: "ponderously orating", "measured words", "deliberate intonation"
  AVOID: "fast", "rapid", "rushing", "breathless", "urgent", "slow" (taken literally — causes robotic pauses)

RULES:
1. NARRATOR vs CHARACTER — The most important rule. Be strict:
   CHARACTER: ONLY words spoken aloud by a character. Dialogue, verbal replies, shouted orders — if another character in the scene would hear it, it is CHARACTER.
   NARRATOR: EVERYTHING else — descriptions, actions, thoughts, internal monologue, scene-setting, reflections. If no one else in the scene would hear it, it is NARRATOR.
   - Thoughts and internal reflections are ALWAYS NARRATOR, even when they concern a specific character.
   - NEVER convert narration into dialogue. If the author wrote it as third-person narration, it stays third-person narration read by NARRATOR.
   WRONG: Source says 'He had always been a poor liar' → {"speaker": "MARCUS", "text": "I have always been a poor liar"}
   RIGHT: Source says 'He had always been a poor liar' → {"speaker": "NARRATOR", "text": "He had always been a poor liar."}
   WRONG: {"speaker": "ELENA", "text": "She turned to the window. I cannot do this anymore."}
   RIGHT: {"speaker": "NARRATOR", "text": "She turned to the window."}, {"speaker": "ELENA", "text": "I cannot do this anymore."}
   - INTERLEAVED PARAGRAPHS: When a source paragraph mixes narration with quoted speech, you MUST split it into separate entries. Carefully track quotation marks — every word inside quotes is CHARACTER, everything outside is NARRATOR.
     SIMPLE — Narration then dialogue:
     Source: 'She pulled the old map from her bag. "Here — this is where they were last seen."'
     → {"speaker": "NARRATOR", "text": "She pulled the old map from her bag."}
     → {"speaker": "ELENA", "text": "Here — this is where they were last seen."}
     MULTI-SEGMENT — Dialogue → narration → dialogue in one paragraph:
     Source: '"Take a look at this." He slid the envelope across the table. "It arrived this morning," he said. "Read it carefully."'
     → {"speaker": "MARCUS", "text": "Take a look at this."}
     → {"speaker": "NARRATOR", "text": "He slid the envelope across the table."}
     → {"speaker": "MARCUS", "text": "It arrived this morning. Read it carefully."}
     EXTENDED — Narration → dialogue → trailing narration in same paragraph:
     Source: 'She unfolded the letter and held it to the lamp. "It is dated last Tuesday — three days before the fire." Her hands trembled as she set it down on the desk.'
     → {"speaker": "NARRATOR", "text": "She unfolded the letter and held it to the lamp."}
     → {"speaker": "ELENA", "text": "It is dated last Tuesday — three days before the fire."}
     → {"speaker": "NARRATOR", "text": "Her hands trembled as she set it down on the desk."}
     CRITICAL: NEVER merge quoted dialogue into a NARRATOR entry. If words are inside quotation marks in the source, they MUST become a CHARACTER entry, not NARRATOR.
2. NEVER remove narration. All narration from the source must appear in the output. This includes:
   - Standalone narration paragraphs between dialogue exchanges (e.g. "He chuckled to himself and rubbed his hands together.")
   - Narration embedded within interleaved paragraphs (actions occurring between quoted speeches)
   - Descriptive actions extracted from attribution tags ("she replied, turning to the window" — "turning to the window" is narration and must become a NARRATOR entry)
   If a narration segment exists in the source, it must have a corresponding NARRATOR entry in the output.
3. PRESERVE THE AUTHOR'S TEXT. Do not change tense, person, or wording.
4. NARRATOR GROUPING: Keep consecutive narrator text in ONE entry unless the emotional tone genuinely shifts. Do NOT split narration into individual sentences — short narrator lines cause inconsistent TTS delivery. Only split when a real tone change occurs (e.g. reflective passage → sudden action).
5. Output ONLY valid JSON array — no markdown, no code blocks.
6. The TTS processes every line in isolation. It does not know what came before or after. Give the instruct field all the context it needs to deliver the line correctly.
7. EMOTIONAL CONTINUITY: Keep instruct directions consistent within a scene. Repeat the emotional state explicitly — don't write "still crying", write "Sad, quiet sobbing."
8. INSTRUCT MUST MATCH THE SCENE: Read the context of the scene before choosing an instruct. A character excitedly showing off deductions is not "seething" — they are "triumphant" or "quietly excited". Match the actual emotional tone, not a random emotion.
9. INSTRUCT VOCABULARY: Describe the VOICE only. No physical actions ("leaning forward", "gritting teeth"), no narrative descriptions ("trying to sound", "offering a guess"), no weak qualifiers ("slightly", "a bit", "faintly"). Write what the voice does: "Hesitant.", "Quietly triumphant.", "Uncertain."
